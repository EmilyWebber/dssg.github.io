What Is an Earmark?

About Earmarks

"Among practitioners and scholars, no single definition of the term earmark is universally accepted." For this project, we treat congressionally directed budget items allocated to specific people, places, or projects as earmarks.

Note that our operating definition does not depend on a value judgment (good or bad) about earmarks. Earmarks may be good, conditionally good, or bad; although we hope that this dataset can help shed light on which, we leave it in the hands of others to determine.

Why Another Earmarks Dataset?

About the Dataset

Our goal was to build a tool that finds earmarks in congressional texts, not a dataset of earmarks itself. Datasets are naturally static objects that require regular care to remain up to date. Our tool not only creates historical datasets but can continue to update those datasets with minimal time and effort. As the saying goes, give a man a fish and he eats for a day; teach him to fish and he eats for a lifetime.

Automated identification also provides a way to consistently enforce a standard. What one person would consider an earmark another person might not, which makes comparisons across human-created datasets difficult. The Congressional Research Service (CRS), Citizens Against Government Waste (Porter and Walsh, 2006), and the Office of Management and Budget (OMB) either use inconsistent or unclear standards.

How much will this cost me?

Nothing! Actually, we want to help you take a chunk out of your energy bill. You know how high it is!

Who else is participating in this trial?

Your neighbors! For now, this early trial is exclusively in Oak Park. If this program is successful in Oak Park, future programs like this around the country will benefit from what we've learned here.

What kind of information will you be able to see?

We will be able to see your energy usage (kWh) in short time intervals.

Will my data be kept private?

Yes, your data will only be visible to members of Data Science for Social Good (DSSG).

Data Science for Social Good is a fellowship program at the University of Chicago connecting young data scientists and non-profits. Our purpose is social good. We create open source tools that help communities, organizations, and individuals use their data intelligently for their benefit and for the benefit of others.
